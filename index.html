A Usability Study of ChatSecure

About

ChatSecure is a mobile messaging app that uses two-way authentication to ensure conversations are kept confidential.
We conducted a usability test and user interviews with 25 participants to evaluate the Android version of ChatSecure.

Get Full Report

  • Role

    Primary Researcher

  • UX Methods

    Usability Testing, Think-a-loud, Interviews

  • Date

    October - December 2013

  • Topic

    Mobile Security & Usability

Findings

  • Unintuitive Feedback

    • 50% of participants did not notice the checkmark that appeared on a lock icon upon successful authentication so they were unaware of the app status.

    • A colour change was also difficult to interpret. Users either did not notice the change or did not associate the colours with secure/insecure states.

  • Inconsistency

    • ChatSecure uses the term 'Fingerprint' to reach a QR code and to refer to a unique alphanumeric string in one particular authentication method.

    • Results showed that all participants had trouble finding their QR code due to this confusing terminology.

  • Unintuitive Behaviour

    • One method of authentication was case-sensitive which was unclear for participants. Therefore authentication mismatches would occur resulting in over 25% of participants making errors when attempting to authenticate.

    • 53% of participants who made errors during this particular authentication method would mistake the carriage return key with the send key therefore sending their authentication code with extra blank spaces.

Many participants had difficulty with authentication regardless of the method used. This is worrisome as this step is crucial for proper Off-The-Record (OTR) communication.

False Successes are especially concerning because users are unaware their communication is unprotected.

Recommendations

ChatSecure's interface violates several design standards which affected participants' ability to develop an accurate mental model of the app, consequently causing some direct security exposures!

Simple changes such as ensuring buttons/menu options clearly communicate their functionality, and improving the clarity in the app's feedback could significantly improve ChatSecure's usability and security.

Publication

Stephanie Hurtado, Ahsan Imran, and Sonia Chiasson. A usability study of ChatSecure. CRA-W Graduate Cohort Workshop, 2014.

An Online Survey of Users' Perception of Privacy Apps

About

This survey was conducted as part of a larger study exploring user perception of privacy apps and the usability of privacy apps. We conducted an online survey with 285 participants to explore users' knowledge of privacy risks as well as their attitudes and motivations to protect their privacy on mobile devices.

Get Full Report

  • Role

    Secondary Researcher

  • UX Methods

    Survey

  • Date

    September 2014 - May 2015

  • Topic

    Usable Security & Privacy

Findings

83% of participants reported they were very or somewhat concerned about their personal information privacy on mobile devices. However, less than 20% of participants reported having used privacy-preserving apps. Out of this subset, only 1/4 thought these apps were useful.

The most common steps taken by participants to protect their privacy were:

  1. Locking Mobile Device
  2. Avoid Sharing Mobile Device

Interestingly, these steps only protect against physical threats not against online threats.

Conclusion

From our surveyed participants, we found users had an incomplete mental model of privacy risks associated with mobile devices. The main reasons were:

  1. Users' knowledge of privacy risks are mediocre at best
  2. Users are not aware of sensitive information they might be leaking through their actions, such as when sharing digital photos

Through our explorations, we found that the majority of users who have previously used privacy-preserving apps did not find them very useful, and that these apps don't help users develop proper mental models of the mitigated risks, so users find little merit in using them. Furthermore, currently available privacy-preserving apps offer poor usability, which could contribute to their low adoption rate.

Publication

Hala Assal, Stephanie Hurtado, Ahsan Imran, and Sonia Chiasson. What’s the deal with privacy apps? A comprehensive exploration of user perception and usability. In Mobile and Ubiquitous Multimedia (MUM). ACM, 2015.

Driver Distraction & Unfamiliar Road Signs

About

The goal of this study was to examine drivers' behaviour when they encounter an unfamiliar situation. If we understood this issue we could design better in-vehicle systems and tools which would improve driver safety, minimize distraction, and provide support to drivers in unfamiliar environments.

Get Full Report

  • Role

    Primary Researcher

  • UX Methods

    User Study, Eye-tracking, Driving Simulation, Quantitative, Qualitative

  • Date

    May 2014 - November 2015

  • Topic

    Automotive Research, Driver Behaviour, Driver Distraction

Study

We conducted two driving-simulation user studies for two driving scenarios (Highway, City). Both scenarios contained three tracks with road signs from one of the following countries:

  • Germany

  • Canada (control group)

  • China

We used a within-subject design to measure driving performance and cognitive workload from 25 participants per study.

Methodology

Each session lasted approximately 45 minutes and unfolded as follows:

  1. Pre-test questionnaire
  2. Eye-tracker calibration and fitting
  3. Practice track
  4. Presentation of actual tracks
  5. Post-test questionnaire

Results

  • Complex Designs and Unfamiliarity

    • Participants fixated longer on road signs with complex design and those that were unfamiliar, which may increase their risk of collision.

  • Misinterpretation

    • Misinterpretation was due to the influence of previous driving experience and local driving practices. This may lead drivers to make errors in judgment putting them further at risk for accidents.

  • Reduced Speeds

    • Participants had a tendency to stay in their lane even when they encountered unfamiliar road signs.

    • Many drivers drove at reduced speeds throughout the track to compensate for the anticipated cognitive load.

Recommendations

Based on our observations and findings we propose two recommendations we believe will improve driver safety, minimize distraction, and provide support for drivers in unfamiliar environments.

  • In-vehicle systems should be used as context-aware interpreters

    • Capable of assisting drivers in an unfamiliar place.

    • Act as interpreters to help drivers understand traffic signs and policies. Familiarity can be used as leverage so a driver can potentially respond to driving situations accordingly, thus reducing driving risks.

  • In-vehicle systems should leverage web mapping services

    • Present drivers with road sign information.

    • Systems should selectively alert drivers about traffic sign policies to prevent overwhelming drivers with notifications. For example, alerting drivers in situations where they attempt to drive in an illegal manner.

Publication

Stephanie Hurtado and Sonia Chiasson. An Eye-tracking Evaluation of Driver Distraction and Unfamiliar Road Signs. In Proceedings of the 8th International Conference on Automotive User Interfaces and Interactive Vehicular Applications. ACM, 2016, Ann Arbor, MI, USA.
